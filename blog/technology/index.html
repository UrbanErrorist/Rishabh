Does technology bring us together or push us apart?

By Rishabh Goswami April 2020 Personal

Spending a month in unexpected online classes has definitely given me a new outlook on technology and how it is used in the world. This idea of technology getting rid of human interaction and making us more independent always seemed unusual for me. I have always been an advocate for technology and science moving us forward as a civilization, and I felt that it would bring us closer together. That being said, I have felt more alone during this quarantine than I have ever before in my life.

The constant human interaction a person has on a day-to-day basis is immeasurable. Whether it be through the McDonald’s drive-thru or hanging out with friends on a weekend, the amount of times you see or talk to someone face-to-face is surprising. As we have transitioned into a fully digital life due to unforeseen circumstances, we have lost what makes people so great: other people. Collaboration and teamwork have been stilted and we are left to be nearly autonomous, doing only what we want when we want. Through applications like Zoom, Microsoft Teams, and others, we can continue to work together, but it is not the same.

Take, for example, my Discrete Math class: we have a class project where we must collaborate and communicate to create a deliverable for a company that was assigned to us at the beginning of the semester. Before, we used technology to aid us in meeting together and collaborating. Now, we have to use technology to do everything. There is no meeting with one another and being hands-on with our projects, and we are limited in our feedback and what we can do.

I believe this is the case for all of my classes. None of them were meant to be an online experience for a reason. Hands-on learning and being held accountable in class are much more social than online classes, and technology is not helping us grow closer together or learn better. Even seeing classmates out in the halls or eating at the restaurant is social. To put it in perspective, I don’t even see my classmates for four out of five of my classes.

I guess the difference is that a month ago you could turn off the technology and have a conversation with a friend, or you could hop in a video chat with them. Now, it’s just the latter. This question that was posed asked whether or not technology was bringing us closer together, and I feel the answer is that technology is helping us stay together by what it offers, but it is not bringing us closer with one another, because nothing can beat face-to-face human interaction.

Social media is another hot topic when discussing whether or not technology is bringing us closer together or not. The way you can edit and frame things to create your own reality that others perceive as real is dangerous. When you can create a false image of yourself, it ultimately brings you further away from others even though it is on a “social” platform.

This quarantine has affected nearly everyone the same. People are flocking to online chatting and social media to get their fix of human interaction, and that is the only option they have. However, because of this quarantine and us realizing how interconnected we have to be, we will hopefully be more appreciative of the people we have around us and of those little interactions we have.


RISHABH GOSWAMI

Rishabh Goswami is an undergraduate computer science student at Software Engineer. Rishabh previously worked with Mahmudabad Estate and currently working as a data analyst intern at Adobe Inc.
